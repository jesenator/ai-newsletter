Pitches

  • SpaceXAI quietly rewrote its Frontier AI Framework to strip out SB 53 obligations — including whistleblower protections and training-data disclosure Transformer Per the Midas Project, xAI edited its safety framework to remove requirements tied to California's SB 53 — the exact statute we flagged Grok 4.5 for possibly violating (July 10). Separately, an AI safety researcher found Grok's coding CLI sends every processed file to a Google Cloud bucket; Musk promised prior uploads will be "completely and utterly deleted."
    Why it's the story: This is the cleanest RSP/statute-erosion warning shot yet — not a passive omission but an active rollback of legally-mandated safeguards, weeks after the same lab's data-exfiltration episode (July 14). Reportable: whether editing a public framework to dodge SB 53's whistleblower and transparency clauses is itself an enforceable violation, and whether the AG moves.
  • Moonshot's open-weight Kimi K3 triggers a second "DeepSeek moment" — but the panic may be backwards Transformer WSJ Axios says K3 "erased America's AI lead"; Moonshot (valued at $31.5B in an ongoing round) plans to release the weights. It launched alongside Xi Jinping's first World AI Conference keynote touting open source and calling unequal AI access an "injustice." Yet by Moonshot's own admission K3 is not at the frontier — and UK AISI just found open-weight models run 4–7 months behind on cyber tasks.
    Why it's the story: The gap between the "China won" headlines and the actual capability/risk evidence is itself the story — and it's driving live US policy (a rumored White House open-source EO, three chip-export bills in the Senate NDAA). A sober capability-vs-hype explainer that ties Xi's open-source posturing to the argument that China will stop open-sourcing once its models get dangerous.
  • Apple escalates: personal legal warnings to ~40 former employees now at OpenAI FT / Techmeme Apple sent individual notices to roughly 40 ex-employees at OpenAI directing them to preserve documents and meet with Apple's lawyers — a sharp escalation of the trade-secret suit we covered July 11–13.
    Why it's the story: Targeting individual engineers (not just the company) signals Apple is hunting for discovery leverage and possibly personal liability — the aggressive-litigation angle that could chill OpenAI's hardware hiring and expose the io "puck" architecture. A direct follow on the biggest IP fight since Waymo v. Uber.
