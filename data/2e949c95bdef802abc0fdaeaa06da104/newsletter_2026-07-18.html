Pitches

  • The anti-data-center movement goes national today — 125 coordinated protests Reuters/Techmeme HumansFirst The grassroots group HumansFirst is staging a "national day of protest" against the "unaccountable" data-center buildout in at least 125 US locations on July 18, demanding politicians protect "hometowns, wallets, and way of life."
    Why it's the story: The AI backlash we've tracked as a political frame (inflation, NY's permit moratorium, the Puck "underpriced issue" thesis) just became a physical, nationwide protest movement — and it lands the same week NY froze permits and Trump called that "a terrible decision." Reportable: who's organizing and funding HumansFirst, whether it overlaps with the extinction-risk Stop AI faction, and whether ratepayer/energy grievances are becoming the backlash's most durable hook.
  • Japan drops 27,500 Nvidia Rubin chips on a sovereign robot foundation model Bloomberg/Techmeme TNW Tokyo will buy 27,500 next-gen Rubin GPUs for a 140MW "AI factory" run by Noetra — a consortium of SoftBank, Sony, NEC and Honda — to build a homegrown "physical AI" model (FRONTia) for robots.
    Why it's the story: A state-backed, robotics-specific sovereign foundation model is a distinct bet from the LLM race, and directly on the robotics beat. It's also a geopolitics story — Japan securing Rubin allocation while Washington gates chips to China, and betting national industrial policy on embodied AI. Reportable: what data Japan's robotics giants pool, how it competes with China's humanoid IPO wave, and whether "physical AI" sovereignty becomes a template other allies copy.
  • China's state fund got voting rights in DeepSeek's round — Tencent and JD got none Bloomberg/Techmeme Sources say China's National AI Industry Investment Fund secured voting rights by joining DeepSeek's $7.4B round, while private backers like Tencent and JD received none.
    Why it's the story: A concrete governance datapoint on how Beijing is quietly acquiring control levers over its frontier crown jewel — exactly as DeepSeek pivots from fierce self-funding to outside capital and a Shanghai IPO (our July 14–15 thread). It sharpens the "will China keep open-sourcing?" debate: state voting power is the mechanism by which Xi's just-announced caution on dangerous models could become binding. Reportable: what the voting rights actually cover (release decisions? weights?) and how it maps onto China's draft frontier-model controls.
