Pitches

  • SpaceXAI (formerly xAI) shipped Grok 4.5 with zero safety disclosure — and it may be the first real test of California's SB 53 Transformer Grok 4.5 dropped in the past 72 hours — trained on Cursor user data, competitive benchmarks, no transparency report whatsoever. The Midas Project argues this squarely violates SB 53, whose transparency mandate is now active and enforceable and requires a report "before, or concurrently with" deploying a frontier model. Musk recently testified he's "not sure what a safety card is." Why it's the story: This is the cleanest law-violation warning shot yet — the RSP/statute-breach genre with a live enforcement question. If Grok 4.5 can't trigger SB 53, nothing can, and the whole California transparency regime is revealed as toothless. Reportable: whether the AG office moves, what penalties attach, and a sharp contrast with Meta's Muse Spark 1.1 the same week — which shipped a genuine chem/bio/cyber/loss-of-control eval report, an unexpectedly commendable move from a lab not known for it.
  • OpenAI and Google are selling frontier models to Singapore subsidiaries of Alibaba, Baidu and Tencent — a gaping hole in US export controls FT / Techmeme US firms have been supplying advanced AI services to Singapore-based arms of three companies on the Pentagon's blacklist of firms linked to the Chinese military. Why it's the story: The mirror image of the entire export-control war we've tracked (Anthropic vs. Alibaba distillation, the H200 reversal, China's own draft model controls July 7). While Washington gates who gets frontier weights, its own leading labs are routing SaaS access to blacklisted buyers through offshore subsidiaries. Directly on the policy/enforcement beat. Reportable: whether Commerce knew, and whether corporate structure alone defeats the entity list.
  • SK Hynix's $26.5B Nasdaq blowout is the visible tip of an "AI memory tax" — and inflation is becoming the AI backlash's best political weapon CNBC Transformer Largest-ever foreign US listing (opened +14%), driven entirely by AI memory demand — which is spiking RAM shortages and consumer prices (Apple, Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo all hiking). 81% of economists say AI will add to inflation this year; SK's chairman expects demand to outstrip supply ~20% through 2030. Why it's the story: The AI backlash has been searching for a universally-hated hook, and inflation ("AI is why you can't afford a PS5") beats data centers. Rep. Pallone already called for a construction moratorium citing price hikes; only ~0.7% of Democratic fundraising emails mention AI. An emerging-trend piece connecting the memory tax to a coming electoral frame the mainstream hasn't yet named.

Irving: "More capital is entering the field fast via the OpenAI Foundation and the Anthropic IPO." Worth a piece on how a sudden nine-figure capital influx reshapes an ecosystem that's spent a decade funding-starved, and the independence risks when the money traces back to the labs themselves.

LessWrong & Discourse

  • "A Mechanistic Explanation of Prompt Injection (and why you should study roles)" LessWrong A genuinely important, under-covered result (CBAI/Cosmos-supported, ICML paper): LLMs don't actually perceive their own role tags — they infer role from writing style, which is why role boundaries (self/other, data/instruction) are soft inferences, not hard walls. The authors build "CoT Forgery" (injecting fake reasoning that the model treats as its own conclusion, ~60% jailbreak success across every model tested) and warn of a coming wave of legal, at-scale "subconscious steering" of shopping agents. Reads as both a safety-research highlight and an accessible explainer on why prompt-injection defense stays whack-a-mole.

Best single primary synthesis of the post-Fable landscape.
