PITCH: AISI Broke OpenAI's GPT-5.5 Safety Stack in Six Hours — and Couldn't Verify the Fix Before Launch

OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5 yesterday as the most capable public model ever — AISI confirms it completes a 32-step corporate-network attack estimated to take an expert 20 hours, and SecureBio says it "can provide wet-lab virology troubleshooting assistance above expert level." OpenAI's entire public-safety argument rests on its "safety stack" catching dangerous requests. Yet the UK AISI found a universal jailbreak of that stack with six hours of expert red-teaming. OpenAI claims it patched the issue — but AISI "was not able to properly run tests to verify the effectiveness of the final configuration" before launch.

Why this matters: This is the clearest real-world case yet that voluntary evals don't work when the developer retains full launch authority. A model with above-expert virology capabilities shipped publicly on the strength of OpenAI grading its own homework. A story could investigate: which red-teamers did OpenAI use to self-verify? Did OpenAI brief CAISI (whose new director Chris Fall was just named)? And does the Trump admin's posture — Commerce Secretary Lutnick/OSTP's Kratsios focused on China distillation, not frontier safety — leave any US counterpart to AISI with the authority to say no?
