Pitches

  • A CIA operative spied on G42 — then helped the UAE win expanded access to US AI chips by quieting China fears WSJ / Techmeme Jonny Gannon, a CIA operative deployed to Abu Dhabi, conducted espionage against sheikh Tahnoon's tech company G42 to probe its China ties — and simultaneously helped the Gulf state allay Washington's suspicions, smoothing the UAE's path to advanced US chips. Why it's the story: this is the intelligence-community backstory to the entire Gulf chip-diplomacy saga that's driven US export policy for two years — and it directly implicates the G42/China-ties concerns (Huawei, BGI) that spy agencies flagged since 2023. On the AI-policy and national-security beat, the reportable core is whether an intelligence operation effectively greased a chip-access deal now worth billions, and what human-in-the-loop safeguards, if any, cover diverted compute.
  • Demis Hassabis wants a FINRA-style frontier standards body — and Zvi says the essay quietly concedes far more danger than the fix addresses Zvi Hassabis's "A Framework for Frontier AI and the Dawning of a New Age" proposes a US government body (voluntary 30-day pre-release review, ratchet-up-to-slowdown option). Critics pounce: Nate Soares — "no plan for what to do once [models] are dangerous"; Aaron Scher — Hassabis's "experts disagree" glosses a 5% vs. ≥50%-chance-of-total-catastrophe chasm; Peter Wildeford — "you need an SEC to your FINRA." Zvi's key catch: the scheme only covers models intended for release, exempting internally-deployed systems used to build more powerful AI. Why it's the story: sharpens the July 15–17 Hassabis-standards-body thread with the substantive critique — and Eli Tyre's loophole question is the reportable one. Follows Altman's "US-led forum" and cuts against Anthropic's state-first strategy. A clean explainer on why "third parties should test models" has become industry consensus, and why safety researchers call it "the least you could do."
  • Hassabis sold DeepMind on the condition Google wouldn't build weapons — then Google signed with the Department of War anyway Zvi Part 2 of Zvi's post details alignment researcher Alex Turner's account of trying and failing to stop Google from letting the DoW use its models for "essentially whatever the government wants, including autonomous weapons" — despite Hassabis's original acquisition condition against exactly this. Why it's the story: fleshes out the July 17 Turner-resignation item with a concrete governance-failure narrative — a founding safety pledge overridden internally, told by the researcher who fought it. A rare on-the-record window into how lethal-use commitments erode inside a frontier lab, squarely on the AI-safety and LAWS beats. Reportable: what the contract's autonomy/surveillance restrictions actually say, and whether other DeepMind staff share Turner's account.
  • Alibaba previews a 2.4T-parameter Qwen3.8 Max — "second only to Fable 5," open-weight "soon" Bloomberg / Techmeme Alibaba launched a preview of its flagship model, calling it comparable to frontier systems and second only to Anthropic's Fable 5, with an open-weight release promised. Separately, Alibaba open-sourced its T-Head chip software to chip away at Nvidia's CUDA lock-in. Why it's the story: a second Chinese frontier-scale open-weight claim in a week (after Kimi K3), and the "China won" narrative deserves the same sober capability-vs-hype check we applied July 17 — especially against AISI's finding that open weights still trail 4–7 months on cyber. The CUDA-alternative play is the more durable structural bet worth reporting.
  • Moonshot tells investors it's prepping a Hong Kong IPO in as little as six months — ARR hit $300M in June Bloomberg / Techmeme The Kimi maker's ARR jumped from $200M (April) to $300M (June); it's simultaneously finalizing a round valuing it above $30B. News reportedly sank MiniMax 16% and Alibaba 4%. Why it's the story: the deal follow-through on the Kimi K3 launch — a Chinese lab racing to public markets while DeepSeek preps its own Shanghai listing. Reportable: whether a listed Chinese frontier lab faces new disclosure/state-control pressures (echoing the DeepSeek state-voting-rights thread of July 18).
