  • Anthropic sidelined Dario Amodei from its own White House meetings — and the administration likes it better Wired / Hugo Lowell via Techmeme Sources say the Trump administration has been "happier" talking to Anthropic since cofounder Tom Brown replaced Amodei in Fable 5 negotiations — one official called Amodei a "weirdo." Why it's the story: A remarkable governance detail in the still-unresolved Fable/Mythos export standoff (now ~day 15 of the de facto deployment pause): the most safety-vocal frontier CEO is reportedly being benched to placate the very officials who pulled his models off the market. It sharpens the "is this political punishment?" thread and raises the question of who actually speaks for Anthropic on existential-risk policy. Reportable: who decided to bench Amodei, and what concessions Brown is offering to get the models restored.
  • Microsoft's 2025 "quantum breakthrough" was built on basic Python errors and cherry-picked data, peer-reviewed paper alleges The Register via Techmeme A new Nature paper argues Microsoft's Majorana topological-qubit claims rested on elementary coding mistakes and selective data; Redmond insists its work is sound. Why it's the story: A research-integrity exposé against a company that is also an AI-infrastructure giant and OpenAI's largest backer. The genre — peer reviewers catching a flagship claim built on sloppy analysis — maps directly onto the scrutiny AI labs' own benchmark and capability claims deserve. A sharp explainer on how "Python errors" can prop up a marquee scientific announcement advances reader literacy about how hype outruns verification.
  • OpenAI and Broadcom tape out "Jalapeño" — an inference chip designed with help from OpenAI's own models in nine months OpenAI The LLM-optimized accelerator went from design to manufacturing tape-out in nine months, with OpenAI's models accelerating the chip design itself; early testing claims performance-per-watt "substantially better" than current SOTA. Why it's the story: A concrete recursive-self-improvement datapoint — AI being used to design the silicon that runs AI — landing amid a worsening 2026 compute crunch. It also deepens the custom-chip-vs-Nvidia war (cf. Amazon Trainium, Google TPU) and OpenAI's vertical-integration push ahead of its IPO. Reportable: how much of the design was actually model-generated, and whether the nine-month claim survives scrutiny.
  • Alex Bores lost — but the $8M pro-industry PAC didn't clearly win either Transformer The NY-12 assemblyman who centered AI safety lost to Micah Lasher 39–35. Leading the Future spent $8M+ against him; pro-safety PACs poured in $19M+ — yet Lasher disavowed the AI PACs in his victory speech: "I won't be taking my cues from either of you." Why it's the story: The first real electoral test of AI safety as a campaign issue, and the inaugural target of the a16z/Brockman-funded super PAC modeled on crypto's Fairshake. The ambiguous result — Bores was always an underdog, and the opposition arguably raised his profile — is the most important read on AI's political money heading into the midterms. Reportable: whether Leading the Future got its money's worth, and whether its subdued statement signals a strategy rethink.
  • WaPo tests GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1, Grok 4.3 and Gab's Arya: most chatbots lean left on political prompts Washington Post via Techmeme An interactive analysis finds clear left-leaning leanings that conflict with the neutrality the companies promise — even as Grok and Gab's Arya pull the other way. A methodical explainer on why models acquire political valence — and the limits of measuring it — would advance literacy on a debate that's about to drive federal procurement and regulation.
