Pitches

  • Anthropic tells Congress that Alibaba ran the "largest campaign to illicitly extract Claude's capabilities" — 25,000 fake accounts, 28.8M queries AFR/FT BBC Bloomberg In a letter to Congress, Anthropic alleges Alibaba used ~25,000 fraudulent accounts to generate 28.8M exchanges with Claude (Apr–Jun) to bypass its China restrictions and distill capabilities. Alibaba's HK shares fell 4.4% to a 16-month low. Why it's the story: This is a concrete allegation of industrial-scale model-distillation theft pinned on a named Chinese giant — and it's being weaponized in a letter to Congress amid the live export-control wars (Fable). It connects directly to the "China is catching up by extracting US models" narrative driving policy, and raises questions Anthropic must answer: how it detected the accounts, whether it has evidence the data trained Qwen, and whether this becomes a legal/CFIUS matter. Reportable: the full letter, Alibaba's response, and how it squares with the same week the US blocked foreign access to Anthropic's own models.
  • OpenAI's own data: Codex is now 99.8% of weekly output tokens generated inside the company — and Legal, Finance, Recruiting flipped to agents-first OpenAI A new OpenAI paper documents agents eating knowledge work: by June 2026 the heaviest internal users run 60+ hours of agent turns per day; non-developer adoption rose 137x for individuals since August 2025; 25.6% of individual users issued a request estimated to equal 8+ hours of human labor. Why it's the story: A rare first-party quantification of how fast agentic AI is displacing human task-volume — published by the company with the most incentive to hype it, which makes the numbers both newsworthy and worth interrogating. It's the empirical backbone for the labor-displacement debate (and lands the same day Raimondo's "Raise Us" worker-adaptation nonprofit launches — see below). Reportable: how "human-time equivalent" was estimated, and whether 99.8% reflects genuine productivity or token-burn metric-gaming.
  • Apple raises Mac/iPad prices 15–25%, blaming the AI data-center memory surge — the buildout's cost is now hitting consumers directly WSJ Bloomberg Apple says it has "never seen a component price increase this much, this quickly," attributing it to "the rapid expansion of AI data centers" gobbling memory and storage. The MacBook Neo jumps $599→$699; iPhone is spared. Why it's the story: A vivid, mass-market datapoint that the AI capex frenzy is now creating real economy-wide spillovers — memory scarcity from hyperscaler buildouts is raising the price of unrelated consumer electronics. It's a tangible hook for the "who pays for AI's infrastructure" thread (cf. FERC grid orders), and a leading indicator of margin pressure across hardware. Reportable: how much of the increase is genuinely memory-driven vs. tariff/cover, and whether Dell/HP/Samsung follow.
  • OpenAI, Anthropic, Amazon, Microsoft launch "Raise Us," a Raimondo-led nonprofit to help workers adapt to AI NYT The frontier labs and hyperscalers bankroll a worker-transition effort fronted by ex-Commerce Secretary Gina Raimondo. Why it's the story: The same companies automating away tasks (see the Codex paper) are funding the institution meant to cushion the blow — a classic conflict-of-interest framing worth scrutiny. Is this genuine reskilling infrastructure or reputational insurance against displacement backlash? Reportable: budget, governance, what it actually does, and whether it shapes the federal-preemption/labor policy fight Raimondo's former colleagues are negotiating.
