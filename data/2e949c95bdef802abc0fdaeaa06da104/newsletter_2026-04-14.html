Pitch: Anthropic's $20M "AI Safety" Super PAC Donation Is a Paper Tiger — and They Knew It

  • Anthropic restricted its $20M donation from being used to influence elections Transformer News Transformer reveals that Anthropic restricted its $20M donation to Public First Action from being used to influence federal elections — despite months of reporting by Bloomberg, the Washington Post, and others assuming the money would fund super PAC political ads for pro-AI-safety candidates. Anthropic only disclosed this restriction after Transformer asked whether the donation complied with laws prohibiting government contractors from making political contributions. Meanwhile, the OpenAI-linked Leading the Future super PAC has disclosed $50M and claims $125M from donors including Greg Brockman and a16z. Public First Action's three super PACs have disclosed only $3.48M in spending. The perception of Anthropic-backed electoral firepower persisted for months — without actually having to deploy it.
  • Why this would be impactful This story exposes a significant information gap in the AI policy landscape. Anthropic — the company that brands itself as the "safety-first" lab — allowed the media and the public to believe it was funding a competitive counterweight to OpenAI-aligned political spending, when its money was legally walled off from elections. This matters because politicians may have taken votes on AI regulation partly based on the assumption that Public First Action had deep Anthropic-backed pockets to protect them from pro-industry challengers. If Public First is actually a "paper tiger" (Transformer's framing), the political calculus around AI safety legislation shifts dramatically heading into the midterms. A story could investigate: Did any members of Congress change their AI policy positions based on the perception of Anthropic-backed electoral support? Did Anthropic's leadership discuss the strategic benefit of the ambiguity internally? And does this undermine the entire notion that there is a well-funded political counterweight to the anti-regulation lobby?
