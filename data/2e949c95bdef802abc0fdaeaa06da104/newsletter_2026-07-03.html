Pitches

  • Trump's AI czar tells the FT: "there will not be an FDA for AI" — and blames the backlash on the industry's own "doomers" Financial Times Sriram Krishnan says the president opposes any centralized AI regulator, casts his job as "fighting China and fighting the doomers," and argues existential-risk framing is an elite overreaction fueling a public backlash that itself endangers US competitiveness. Favors post-hoc liability and market competition over any approval gate.
    Why it's the story: This is the clearest on-record articulation yet of the administration's anti-regulator posture — and it directly contradicts the reality on the ground, where the White House has spent three weeks gating Fable, Mythos and GPT-5.6 access customer-by-customer (a de facto licensing regime). The "no FDA / blame the doomers" line is the political cover for the opaque case-by-case system, and it sharpens the IVO/independent-auditor debate we covered July 2. Reportable: reconcile "no regulator" rhetoric with the cyber apparatus already deciding who gets frontier models, and whether Krishnan endorses the ad hoc bans he's rhetorically against.
  • Alibaba bans Claude Code internally and orders employees to wipe all Claude models — as Anthropic moves to slam shut China's workaround loopholes The Information FT Same morning: Alibaba banned Claude Code and told staff to remove all Claude models from work computers over Anthropic "security concerns," while the FT reports Anthropic is closing the cloud-provider and overseas-subsidiary loopholes that let Chinese firms like Ant keep using its models despite restrictions.
    Why it's the story: The escalation from Anthropic's distillation accusations (June 25) into a mutual decoupling — Alibaba purging Claude while Anthropic hunts down proxy access — is the corporate front of the export-control war, and a concrete test of whether model-level geofencing is enforceable (see the Telegram Claude resale market, June 28). Reportable: what "security concerns" Alibaba cites, whether this is retaliation for the distillation allegations, and which loopholes Anthropic can actually plug.
  • Meta's next model, "Watermelon," matches GPT-5.5 and burns "an order of magnitude more compute than Avocado," Alexandr Wang tells staff Business Insider Meta's superintelligence chief told employees the model now in training equals GPT-5.5 and uses ~10x the compute of its predecessor.
    Why it's the story: First internal signal that Meta's post-reorg supercluster spend is producing a near-frontier result — but only matching a model OpenAI has already surpassed with Sol, raising the question of whether Meta's compute-for-capability ratio is competitive. Pairs with Meta's new cloud-business pivot (July 1) and its distillation-contamination worries (June 29). Reportable: the actual compute figures, and how "matches GPT-5.5" was benchmarked internally.
  • Anthropic's IPO machinery cranks up: bankers hire Freshfields for a listing that could top a $1T valuation The Information The UK firm — which advised on Google/Wiz and ServiceNow/Armis — has landed a role on an IPO expected to raise tens of billions at a $1T+ valuation.
    Why it's the story: A safety-branded lab pursuing a trillion-dollar public listing while it's still subject to government access controls (Mythos was dark for three weeks) is a governance and disclosure story in its own right — what risk factors does an RSP-committed company file, and how does the licensing regime get disclosed to public investors? Lands as OpenAI reportedly delays its own IPO (June 26).
