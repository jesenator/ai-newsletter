Pitch: Federal Judge Rules AI Chats Have No Attorney-Client Privilege — and Lawyers Are Scrambling

Reuters reports today that U.S. law firms are racing to warn clients: your conversations with Claude and ChatGPT can be used against you in court. The trigger was a February ruling by Judge Jed Rakoff (SDNY) in United States v. Heppner, ordering the former CEO of bankrupt GWG Holdings to hand over 31 documents he generated using Anthropic's Claude to prepare reports for his defense lawyers. Rakoff: "No attorney-client relationship exists or could exist, between an AI user and a platform such as Claude." Anthropic and OpenAI's own terms state users have no expectation of privacy.

Why this matters: This is a "warning shot" story with enormous real-world consequences. Over 50% of U.S. workers now use AI (Gallup, covered Apr 13). Many are likely using ChatGPT or Claude for legal questions — drafting demand letters, preparing for depositions, analyzing contracts. The Rakoff ruling means all of those conversations are potentially discoverable evidence. More than a dozen major law firms have issued advisories. Sher Tremonte now warns in client contracts that "disclosure of privileged communications to a third-party AI platform may constitute a waiver of the attorney-client privilege." A story could investigate: How many people are using AI chatbots for legal advice without understanding this risk? Do Anthropic and OpenAI have any obligation to warn users that their conversations could be subpoenaed? And could this ruling chill legitimate AI use in legal settings — or force labs to build privileged-communication modes?
