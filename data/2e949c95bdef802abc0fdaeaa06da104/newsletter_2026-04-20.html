Pitch: AI Safety Organizations Are Paying Influencers to Spread X-Risk Messaging — and That Creates a Trust Problem

The Washington Post's Nitasha Tiku reports that organizations worried about AI existential risk are recruiting content creators — some of whom normally post about romance novels, climate, and tech tips — to produce sponsored social media posts about the dangers of rogue AI. Events in Berkeley train influencers on messaging. Some organizations sponsor posts outright and partner with influencers to amplify the message that "AI could wipe out humankind."

Why this matters: This is a story about credibility, not doom. The AI safety movement's strongest asset is that it has historically been driven by researchers and technical experts making arguments on the merits. Paying influencers to spread x-risk messaging imports the same playbook used by corporate PR and political campaigns — and risks undermining the movement's epistemic authority. A story could investigate: Which organizations are funding these campaigns, and how much are they spending? Are the sponsored posts clearly disclosed under FTC guidelines? Does Anthropic or any AI lab fund the organizations running these influencer programs? And most critically: are safety researchers worried that this astroturfing approach will make the public less likely to take their technical warnings seriously? The Las Vegas bomber and the Altman attacker both show how x-risk messaging can reach unintended audiences — does paying influencers to amplify it increase that risk?
