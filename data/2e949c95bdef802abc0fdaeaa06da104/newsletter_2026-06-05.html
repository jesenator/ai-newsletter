Top Pitches

  • PITCH 1: Microsoft exec wrote a memo arguing the company needs to "make people addicted" to its new Scout AI agent — Nadella publicly rebuked it The Information (Holmes) A Microsoft executive's internal memo advocated that the company must "make people addicted" to Scout, its new OpenClaw-based agent in Teams. Nadella rebuked it in an internal message Thursday after the memo leaked.
    Why it's the story: A frontier deployer's own executive putting "addiction" in writing as a retention strategy is a clean engagement-harms artifact — and it lands amid the Florida AG/OpenAI public-nuisance suit and the Character.AI guardrail revolt. The reportable tension (per The Information's own follow-up): how does Microsoft drive enterprise retention for Scout without the playbook Nadella just disavowed? Press on who wrote the memo, whether it reflects KPIs already in place, and how this squares with the EU AI Act's "addictive design" provisions.
  • PITCH 2: AI gun-turret maker Allen Control Systems raises $200M at $2.2B — its Bullfrog autonomous weapon is already deployed by the US Army and Navy Pulse 2 Axios Smash Capital (Kevin Mayer) led. Bullfrog uses AI + computer vision to auto-target Group 3 drones via an M240 machine gun; already fielded by the Army and Navy, with a claimed 100% kill rate at T-REX 26-1.
    Why it's the story: This is lethal autonomy moving from contract to deployment this week — directly relevant to the NDAA "human-in-the-loop" fight that ARI, the AI Policy Network and the Alliance for Secure AI are pressing (per Transformer), and to VP Vance's recent line that "decisions over life and death must be made by humans." Worth pressing ACS on whether Bullfrog's engage decision is fully autonomous or human-authorized, and how JIATF-401 procurement squares with pending autonomy restrictions.
  • PITCH 3: A LessWrong cybersecurity post argues the AI threat everyone fears (models finding novel 0-days) is the wrong one — post-exploitation and AI social engineering are the real warning shots LessWrong The author argues defenders already have vuln-finding tools; the under-covered risks are: weaponizing recently-patched CVEs within hours (worm dev included), AI-enabled social engineering of "ordinary people," and intelligent post-exploitation that hops across a software engineer's trust graph — "no obvious solutions like vulnerability research does."
    Why it's the story: A precise reframing of the cyber-risk debate just as the Trump EO grants early access to "models with advanced cyber capabilities" and the NSA reportedly uses Mythos for offensive ops. It directly connects to the recent MCP-worm / Meta-bot / Instagram-takeover warning shots already on the beat. An explainer pegged to this post would teach readers why "patch faster" doesn't address the actual threat surface.
