PITCH: Google Is Quietly Writing the Chatbot "Child Safety" Bills That Would Exempt Google

  • Transformer investigation reveals coordinated state legislation with industry carve-outs Transformer News Six states (CO, HI, AZ, GA, NE, ID) have introduced structurally identical chatbot safety bills modeled on Oregon's SB 1546 — but with new carve-outs that exempt chatbots "housed within another service," which would shield Google and Meta from compliance. Google has voiced support in HI, NE, and AZ. A Google rep suggested changes to Hawaii's bill text during a hearing on Wednesday. The Tech Oversight Project's Marjorie Connolly: "Tech lobbyists supply full bill text and amendments, then use proxy groups to manufacture the appearance of local support." The bills also eliminate private right of action in most cases, limiting enforcement to underfunded state AGs. Safety advocates call it a "technical loophole" that lets companies evade child protections for unregistered users.
  • Meanwhile, the White House released its own AI framework today — also calling for state preemption Politico/Yahoo The White House framework explicitly calls on Congress to preempt state AI laws that regulate model development or penalize companies for downstream use. It includes age-gating requirements and asks Congress not to create any new AI regulatory agencies. Sen. Cruz hopes to merge this with KOSA by end of April.
  • Why this matters There's a two-pronged strategy emerging: at the federal level, the White House wants to override state AI regulation entirely. At the state level, Google-backed bills are passing that look like child safety laws but contain carve-outs that would exempt the biggest AI companies. The net result: states that pass these bills may think they've "fixed the problem" while locking in protections that don't cover the most widely used chatbots. This is the exact playbook the Build American AI investigation exposed last week, but applied to child safety legislation. A story could map the exact provenance of the bill text, identify which lobbyists or trade groups drafted the carve-out language, and test whether Google's own products would be exempt.
