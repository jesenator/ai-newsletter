Pitches

  • The AI 2027 team is back with "AI 2040: Plan A" — a prescriptive scenario where the US and China deliberately delay superintelligence by a decade AI Futures Project ai-2040.com Kokotajlo & co. dropped their year-in-the-making follow-up today. Unlike AI 2027's forecast, this is a recommendation: superintelligence would have arrived in 2030, but "decisive action" by both governments pushes it to 2040.
    Why it's the story: AI 2027 shaped an enormous amount of policy discourse and press. Its authors explicitly pivoting from prediction to normative planning — and staking out that a US–China slowdown is "plausible enough to aim for" — is itself news, and a natural hook for interrogating whether their proposed governance mechanisms (verification, compute controls, coordination with Beijing) are realistic given the actual on-the-ground licensing chaos of the past month. First-mover interview opportunity with Kokotajlo on what changed his mind about tractability.
  • Meta declares a price war: Meta Model API at ~25% the cost of OpenAI/Anthropic, plus a homegrown "Iris" chip entering production in September Bloomberg Reuters The Verge Zuckerberg says pricing will be "aggressive and attractive" — Muse Spark 1.1 (a "step-change" coding model) lists at $1.25/$4.25 per 1M tokens. Same day: an internal memo says Meta starts making its Iris chip in September, targeting 14GW of compute by 2027.
    Why it's the story: Meta is attacking the two economic pillars of the frontier labs at once — undercutting API margins and reducing its Nvidia dependence with custom silicon. This is the most concrete sign yet that the model layer is commoditizing, directly relevant to the "is this a bubble" thread and to OpenAI/Anthropic's trillion-dollar valuation math. Reportable: whether 25% pricing is sustainable or subsidized, and how Iris squares with Meta's earlier "Watermelon" compute-for-capability struggles (July 3).
  • Bun's creator says he rewrote the runtime from Zig to Rust in 11 days with a pre-release Claude Fable 5 — a year of work for three engineers bun.com (Jarred Sumner) The disclosure: Bun was acquired by Anthropic in December 2025, and the team works there.
    Why it's the story: This is a vivid, quantified capability datapoint feeding the "age of humans writing code is ending" narrative — but the buried lede is the conflict of interest. An Anthropic employee using unreleased Anthropic models to produce a viral marketing claim about Anthropic's own product deserves scrutiny about verifiability and pre-release access. Pairs with OpenAI's own new warning (below) that coding benchmarks are unreliable.
  • OpenAI publishes its national-security doctrine — and, separately, guts a rival's coding benchmark OpenAI OpenAI OpenAI laid out "principles for responsible AI use, democratic accountability, and public safety" in government/defense partnerships, and released an analysis finding reliability problems in SWE-Bench Pro.
    Why it's the story: The government post lands squarely amid the DOD–Anthropic lethal-use fight (July 7) and Altman's "US-led forum" push (July 2) — worth parsing what OpenAI's stated red lines actually forbid versus enable. The benchmark critique is an under-covered AI-literacy angle: the industry's headline capability claims (see Bun, above) rest on evals OpenAI itself now says are noisy.
