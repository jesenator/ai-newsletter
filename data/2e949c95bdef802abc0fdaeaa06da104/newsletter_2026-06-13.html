Top Pitches

  • PITCH 1: Washington just made a frontier model disappear — a de facto licensing regime, born by accident Transformer Anthropic The Verge Friday night, Commerce issued an export-control directive suspending all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 by any foreign national, inside or outside the US — including Anthropic's own foreign-national employees. Anthropic says the only way to comply was to disable both models for every customer, and calls the order a "misunderstanding" it's working to reverse. Axios: the admin had tried to get Anthropic to pause the release, failed, then acted after Amazon researchers reported a jailbreak of Fable's cyber capabilities. Luta Security's CEO calls it a "complete overreaction." Why it's the story: This is the single most consequential AI-policy event of the year so far. As Transformer argues, the government used export-control law — not any statutory AI authority or agreed danger threshold — to force a deployed model off the market entirely. That's an ad hoc, post-hoc licensing regime, exactly what the administration claims to oppose, established by a jailbreak report rather than a testing process. Connects every beat thread at once: the prior-restraint EO, CAISI's silencing, NSPM-11, and the fraught government–Anthropic relationship (was this safety, or punishing a company the White House dislikes?). Reportable: what statute they're actually citing, whether "export controls over US persons" is legally sound, and what "hardening the national security apparatus in a few weeks" means for restoration.
  • PITCH 2: A coalition of 42 state AGs subpoenas OpenAI — a sweeping probe into its "impact on users" WSJ Bloomberg AA OpenAI was served Friday with a subpoena from a coalition of 42 state attorneys general seeking documents across "a wide range of its activities and impact on users." Why it's the story: A 42-state bloc is a fundamentally different threat than the standalone Florida nuisance suit — it's the kind of multistate consumer-protection coalition that reshaped Big Tobacco and Meta/Instagram. Lands days after OpenAI's confidential S-1 and Altman's "IPO within a year" message, putting a major litigation overhang on a retail-heavy mega-listing. Reportable: which AGs are leading, whether the focus is teen-safety/mental-health harms (the murder/suicide suits) or the for-profit conversion, and how this dovetails with the state-preemption fight the White House is brokering.
  • PITCH 3: Inside Meta's "soul-crushing" Applied AI team — the human cost of Zuckerberg's chaotic Superintelligence pivot Wired Sources describe deep frustration on the Applied AI team — formed in March to support Superintelligence Labs — over menial projects and "soul-crushing" work, amid executives and employees struggling with Meta's "chaotic AI strategy." Why it's the story: Meta spent a fortune on $100M+ comp packages to assemble a superintelligence dream team; this is the morale-and-attrition counter-narrative, and a leading indicator of where the next wave of talent flows (River AI, Anthropic, etc.). Pairs with this week's stalled-Rivos-chip reporting as evidence the Meta AI reorg is faltering operationally. Reportable: who's leaving, whether the Applied/SI Labs split is producing turf wars, and how it squares with Meta's public capex bullishness.
