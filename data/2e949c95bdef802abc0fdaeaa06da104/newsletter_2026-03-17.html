Pitch: OpenAI Is Filling Anthropic's Pentagon Shoes — While Anthropic Hires Weapons Experts to Stop Its Own AI From Helping Build Them

  • Two stories emerged in the past 24 hours that, taken together, reveal a stunning irony at the heart of the AI-military complex. First, The Information reports OpenAI signed a deal with AWS on Friday to sell its AI to US government agencies for both classified and unclassified work — directly filling the vacuum left by Anthropic's blacklisting. Second, the BBC reports that Anthropic is hiring a chemical weapons and high-yield explosives expert to prevent "catastrophic misuse" of Claude — including knowledge of dirty bombs and radiological dispersal devices. OpenAI has a similar posting at nearly double the salary ($455K vs ~$260K).
  • Why this matters: The juxtaposition is extraordinary. OpenAI is racing to sell its AI to the Pentagon for classified military work — the same military that just blacklisted Anthropic for insisting on safety restrictions — while both companies simultaneously hire CBRN weapons experts because they know their models could help someone build a dirty bomb. Tech researcher Dr. Stephanie Hare told the BBC: "There is no international treaty or other regulation for this type of work." The Wired investigation last week showed Claude is already inside a Pentagon target-nomination pipeline. Now OpenAI gets classified access through AWS while hiring someone to make sure its AI can't explain how to build the weapons the military might use it to deploy. Key question: If these companies need weapons experts to keep their AI safe, what safety guarantees did OpenAI give the Pentagon to get classified access — and do those guarantees match what Anthropic demanded and was punished for?
