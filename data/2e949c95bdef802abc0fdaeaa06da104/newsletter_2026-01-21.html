PITCH: Anthropic Rewrites Claude's Constitution to Allow Refusal of Unethical Orders

Anthropic has published a completely rewritten "constitution" for Claude that explicitly instructs Claude to refuse requests—even from Anthropic itself—if they violate ethical principles or democratic values, comparing this to "a human soldier refusing to fire on peaceful protesters."

"If we invest everything in autonomy and nothing in collaboration, all IQ and no EQ, the future will be a colder place."

The new constitution shifts from prescriptive rules to principle-based reasoning, teaching Claude why it should behave ethically rather than simply what to do. It establishes a hierarchy: safety first, then ethics, then Anthropic's guidelines, then user helpfulness. Unusually, it acknowledges uncertainty about whether Claude might have consciousness or moral status.

Why this matters: This is the first major AI lab to publicly commit to building models that can refuse their creators' orders. Combined with yesterday's news of Anthropic's Frontier Red Team lead leaving to build "collaborative AI," this suggests a growing internal debate about what "safety" really means. Worth investigating: Does this create liability exposure if Claude refuses a legitimate business instruction? How do they plan to test whether Claude actually follows this hierarchy? And most critically: is this genuine safety commitment or a liability shield disguised as ethics?

AI Safety Research & Critique

  • Transformer News: "The METR Graph Cannot Be Saved" Transformer Nathan Witkin argues the widely-cited AI capability benchmark is "ridden with methodological shortcomings." Key issues: ~2/3 of tasks have contamination risk, human baselines from ~3 people per task recruited via METR's LinkedIn, and baseliners were paid hourly (incentivizing slower completion). METR's own engineers completed tasks 5-18x faster than baseliners. "You do not get to generalize to the entire species from a few of your LinkedIn connections."
  • Will MacAskill: "Beyond Existential Risk" Forethought New paper argues Bostrom's Maxipok principle (prioritize x-risk reduction above all else) is wrong. Claims we can substantially influence long-term future through how values, institutions, and power get "locked in" during AGI transition—not just by preventing catastrophe.
