PITCH: OpenAI's Safety Team Flagged the Tumbler Ridge Shooter 8 Months Before the Attack — Leadership Overruled Them to Protect the IPO

Seven families filed suit in N.D. Cal. today alleging that roughly 12 OpenAI employees identified shooter Jesse Van Rootselaar's account as "a credible and specific threat of gun violence against real people" eight months before the Feb. 10 attack, urged Altman and senior leaders to notify Canadian authorities, and were overruled (Guardian, BBC). The complaint — filed by Jay Edelson — alleges the concealment was motivated by "corporate survival" and protecting the $1T IPO. OpenAI deactivated the account but left intact the documented path to make a new one, which Van Rootselaar followed. Altman sent a private apology letter to Tumbler Ridge last Friday; Premier Eby called it "grossly insufficient."

Why this is the story: This is the first frontier-lab whistleblowing-adjacent mass-casualty case where employees reportedly told the CEO, in writing, about an imminent threat. It transforms the Tumbler Ridge narrative from "model harm" to documented internal override of the safety team — a governance story parallel to the AGI-clause retirement two days ago. A reporter at The Information could: (1) identify the 12 employees and whether any have left or spoken to the WSJ; (2) obtain the internal threat-escalation policy in force in June 2025; (3) ask whether Altman's apology letter constitutes an admission against interest in the civil suits and the live Florida criminal probe; (4) probe whether OpenAI's S-1 must now disclose the safety-override as a material risk; and (5) tie this to the FSU shooter subpoena — two mass shootings, same pattern, one company.
