Pitches

  • Alex Turner goes on the record: the full inside account of how DeepMind's founding "no weapons" pledge collapsed Transformer The alignment researcher who quit DeepMind (flagged July 17/19) now details his failed campaign: a 25-page counter-proposal of contract language and oversight mechanisms sent to Hassabis that senior policy staff never returned; getting Jeff Dean to co-sign an amicus brief backing Anthropic against the Pentagon; and Google signing a classified deal — reportedly with fewer restrictions than OpenAI's — that he learned about at 11:45pm via Signal, never announced internally.
    Why it's the story: This is a rare, named, first-person governance-failure narrative from inside a frontier lab — the strongest artifact yet on how a "trust, not trustless" safety bet (Hassabis's own framing) produced zero binding concessions at the clearest red line. Turner's thesis is directly reportable and quotable: "society cannot rely on ethics-motivated people standing firm… we need structures: binding contracts, independent auditors." Reportable next steps: what the classified DoW contract's autonomy/surveillance language actually says, whether Dean threatened to walk, and Stuart Russell's IASEAI's unfulfilled promised member poll.
  • Bessent says the US could "sanction" Chinese AI models over distillation CNBC / Techmeme Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the administration will look into whether Chinese models were built via unauthorized distillation and that it could "sanction them."
    Why it's the story: This is the enforcement escalation of two threads on your beat — the $6B/year "adversarial distillation" loss estimate (July 13) and the reviving open-model ban (July 20). Sanctioning a model (vs. a company or chip) would be a novel legal mechanism worth interrogating: what statutory authority, does it target Moonshot/DeepSeek specifically ahead of their HK/Shanghai IPOs, and the safety counter — distilled clones inherit capability while shedding guardrails, so a ban on weights doesn't touch the underlying risk while entrenching OpenAI/Anthropic. Lands the same day Moonshot preps a $50B final raise.
  • Google ships "Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber" — a vuln-hunting model handed first to governments — and starts its "most ambitious pre-training run yet" for Gemini 4 DeepMind The Verge / Techmeme Flash Cyber integrates into CodeMender to identify and patch vulnerabilities, pitched as a cheap alternative to models like Mythos and available first to governments and select partners. Google also confirmed Gemini 4 pre-training has begun.
    Why it's the story: A dedicated dual-use cyber model gated to governments is a concrete offense/defense case study — exactly the capability class AISI's open-weight cyber-gap findings (4–7 months) are measuring. Reportable: was Flash Cyber run through any frontier-safety/misuse evals given its offensive potential, which governments get access, and how "patch vulnerabilities" cuts both ways. The "most ambitious pre-training run yet" line is also a scaling-still-works datapoint amid the RSI/data-bottleneck debate.
  • Humanoid becomes Europe's first pure-play humanoid-robotics unicorn — $152M Series A at $1.35B Reuters Forbes The UK firm's raise — the largest-ever Series A for a humanoid-first company — was led by Prime Movers Lab (a Figure AI backer) with Schaeffler, Bosch and Fubon; total funding now $270M for wheeled industrial humanoids.
    Why it's the story: Directly on the robotics beat and the clearest sign yet that "physical AI" capital has crossed the Atlantic, echoing the China humanoid IPO wave (LimX, AI² Robotics) and Japan's sovereign robot model (July 18). Industrial suppliers Bosch/Schaeffler on the cap table hint at built-in deployment sites — reportable which factory floors go live first, and whether wheeled (vs. biped) is becoming the industrial consensus (cf. Walden Robotics, July 15).
