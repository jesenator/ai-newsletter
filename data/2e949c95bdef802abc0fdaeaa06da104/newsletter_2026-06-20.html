Pitches

  • Nobel laureate John Jumper — the AlphaFold pioneer — is leaving Google DeepMind for Anthropic Bloomberg Business Insider X: Andreas Kimmonismus After nearly nine years, the man who shared the 2024 Nobel in Chemistry with Demis Hassabis is decamping to Anthropic (after "some time to recharge"). Bloomberg frames him as a key member of Google's AI coding team, deepening the talent drain to Anthropic/OpenAI ahead of their IPOs.
    Why it's the story: A Nobel-winning Hassabis collaborator defecting to a rival lab is the most symbolically loaded talent move of the year — a science-for-medicine icon joining the most safety-branded frontier lab. Reportable: what he'll actually build (bio? alignment? science agents?), how it interacts with Anthropic's dual-use bio posture, and what it signals about DeepMind retention as Google scrambles to match Anthropic/OpenAI on coding.
  • Blackburn is about to introduce the grand bargain: child-safety + deepfake protections traded for state-AI-law preemption Transformer Politico Transformer reports Blackburn's White House negotiations have "progressed" and the package will land "soon," bundling KOSA, NO FAKES, and the App Store Accountability Act in exchange for some form of preemption over state AI laws — possibly adding the GUARD Act (chatbot liability for minors). NO FAKES cleared Senate Judiciary Thursday but drew speech objections from three Republicans and a Democrat, including Ted Cruz.
    Why it's the story: This is the legislative vehicle that could finally federally preempt the 1,000+ state AI bills — packaged so it's politically painful to oppose. The horse-trade (kids' safety for industry's top regulatory ask) deserves scrutiny: which harms get codified, which state protections die, and whether Cruz's rival preemption ambitions blow it up. Bill text is being held tight — get it.
  • Hyundai moves to fully own Boston Dynamics, buying out SoftBank's last 9.65% for $325M Reuters The deal would make the US humanoid pioneer a wholly owned Hyundai subsidiary, completing SoftBank's exit. Lands amid a surge of robotics capital (NEURA's €1.2B, Genesis AI's $105M, Standard Bots' $200M).
    Why it's the story: Full consolidation of the most storied US robotics name under a Korean automaker — a clean lens on who owns the humanoid stack as "physical AI" funding froths. Reportable: the implied valuation vs. the $1.1B 2020 deal, what it means for Atlas commercialization, and whether US-origin robotics IP under foreign ownership draws any CFIUS or procurement attention given the "ban Chinese robots" push.
