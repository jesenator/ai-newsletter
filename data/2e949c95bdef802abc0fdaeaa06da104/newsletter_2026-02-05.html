PITCH: AI Safety Startup Goodfire Removes Non-Disparagement Clauses—After Raising $150M

Goodfire raised $150M at $1.25B valuation to decode AI models—but the same week, CEO Eric Ho had to remove lifetime non-disparagement agreements from all employee contracts after former employee Liv raised concerns publicly. The clauses prevented employees from commenting on anything beyond minimum legal whistleblower protections—and notably wouldn't have covered safety whistleblowing.

"I raised this issue extensively internally. Further, I was asked to sign a new confidential non-disparagement agreement, which would have prevented me from raising this publicly." — Liv, former Goodfire employee

Why this matters: An AI interpretability company—whose entire mission is making AI systems transparent—was silencing employees about internal practices. The gap between Goodfire's public mission (understanding what's happening inside AI) and its employment practices (preventing staff from speaking) is striking. Multiple AI safety researchers publicly stated they won't recommend Goodfire for work or events until this is explained. This story connects AI safety governance to internal corporate culture in a way that deserves scrutiny. Worth investigating: Are other AI safety startups using similar clauses? And what exactly was Goodfire trying to prevent employees from discussing?
