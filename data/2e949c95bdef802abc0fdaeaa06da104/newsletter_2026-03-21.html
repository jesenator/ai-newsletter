Pitch: Anthropic's New Filing Reveals What the Pentagon Actually Fears About AI Safety Restrictions

  • In a new court filing reported by Wired (Paresh Dave), Anthropic says it technically cannot manipulate Claude once the military has deployed it, directly denying the DOD's allegation that Anthropic could "tamper with models during war." Anthropic's argument: deployed model weights are static copies under the military's control — the company has no remote kill switch or backdoor.

    This filing is significant because it reveals the DOD's core fear: that an AI vendor with ethical commitments could disable or alter its AI during active warfighting operations. Anthropic is now forced to argue that it is technically incapable of doing the very thing AI safety researchers might want it to be able to do — intervening when a deployed model is misused. The Pentagon's position, stripped bare, is: "Any company that cares about safety is a sabotage risk." Meanwhile, a leaked DOD letter from Deputy Secretary Feinberg (March 9) designates Palantir's Maven as an official program of record — locking in long-term funding for the AI targeting system that runs on Claude, even as the Pentagon tries to ban Anthropic.

    The contradiction is extraordinary: the Pentagon is simultaneously institutionalizing a system built on Claude while trying to blacklist Claude's maker. A story could map the technical claims in both filings and test whether Anthropic truly cannot modify deployed models — and whether the Pentagon's "sabotage" theory has any engineering basis. Hearing is Monday.
