Pitches

  • An AI startup is sending camera-clad cleaners into NYC apartments for free — to harvest "tonnes" of in-home data for training robots BBC Micro AGI's "Shift" program puts head-cam-wearing cleaners and chefs into people's homes ~5x/day, recording every surface and hand motion. The business model: sell the anonymized in-home footage to robotics labs. EFF and EPIC call it "a diabolically creative way to sell privacy invasion." Why it's the story: A vivid, on-the-ground look at the rawest input in the robotics race — dexterous-manipulation data — and the surveillance bargain underwriting it. Connects the embodied-AI funding froth (NEURA, Boston Dynamics, Genesis) to a concrete consumer-harm/consent question mainstream tech press hasn't probed. Reportable: who's actually buying the data, what "anonymized" means for in-home video, and whether the cleaners realize they're training their replacements.
  • FERC just issued "show cause" orders to all six non-Texas US grid operators over AI data-center demand via Perplexity Under Section 206 of the Federal Power Act, FERC is forcing every major RTO (except ERCOT) to justify how it plans and prices for the GPU-data-center load surge — a foundational move to reshape who pays for AI's grid strain. Why it's the story: This is the regulatory other-shoe to the data-center backlash beat — instead of county zoning boards, it's the federal energy regulator stepping in on cost allocation. The buildout narrative (hyperscaler capex, the xAI/Grok turbine fight) ultimately runs through who absorbs the power bill. Reportable: whether ratepayers or hyperscalers get stuck with the tab, and how this interacts with the DOJ's "Grok is mission-critical" turbine defense.
  • Big Tech and startups are openly hyping "humanizer" and "autotyper" apps that defeat AI-detection software NYT / Techmeme A new NYT investigation: companies are using social ads to push tools that slowly auto-type AI essays and rewrite text to sound less robotic — explicitly to trick teachers and detectors. Why it's the story: A concrete, fast-growing harm category sitting at the intersection of the education-AI fights (Norway's near-ban, covered June 19) and the limits of AI detection. An explainer on why detection is fundamentally losing — and who profits from selling evasion — advances AI literacy and has clear policy hooks.
  • Inside Palantir's fight to keep its £330M, seven-year NHS England contract as a 2027 break clause looms FT / Techmeme The FT details mounting public and political pressure to kill the deal, with Palantir complaining about "the politicisation of procurement." Why it's the story: The clearest live case study of the sovereignty/trust backlash against US AI infrastructure inside public health systems — directly resonant with the post-Fable "can we depend on US-governed AI?" debate. Reportable: how the break clause works, what data-governance concessions Palantir is offering, and whether this becomes a template for other European governments.
