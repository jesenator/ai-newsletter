PITCH: Microsoft and OpenAI Quietly Retired the "AGI Clause" — The Single Most Important Governance Mechanism in AI Just Vanished

This morning Microsoft and OpenAI amended their partnership, removing the clause that would have cut off Microsoft's IP access upon an OpenAI board declaration of AGI (The Information, WSJ). Microsoft's license now runs through 2032, is non-exclusive, and OpenAI can ship on any cloud. Microsoft stops paying OpenAI a revenue share; OpenAI's revenue share to Microsoft continues to 2030 with a cap.

Why this is the story: The AGI clause was the only contractual tripwire in the OpenAI–Microsoft relationship that gave the nonprofit board meaningful leverage to halt commercialization if it judged a model too dangerous. By retiring it in exchange for "long-term clarity," OpenAI has effectively priced out its last remaining safety-governance checkpoint. Pair this with Sam Altman's new "Our Principles" post from Sunday — which replaces the old mission-first framing with "democratization/empowerment/universal prosperity/resilience/adaptability" — and you have a coordinated rewrite of OpenAI's governance identity in under 24 hours. A story could: (1) ask which OpenAI board members signed off on dissolving the AGI trigger; (2) probe whether the new "Resilience" principle creates any binding halt condition, or is purely aspirational; (3) compare to Anthropic's RSP v3, which Zvi argued already broke prior commitments; and (4) ask whether this removes the last contractual justification for OpenAI's nonprofit structure in ongoing AG reviews (CA, DE).
