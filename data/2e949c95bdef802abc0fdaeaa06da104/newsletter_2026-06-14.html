Top Pitches

  • The Fable shutdown becomes a he-said/he-said — Sacks says Amodei "refused" to patch; Semafor says it was really about suspected Chinese access Tom's Hardware Semafor The Information The story we pitched Saturday has hardened into competing narratives. David Sacks now says a trusted partner found a jailbreak bridging Fable to Mythos's "unrestricted cyber capabilities," that the admin asked Amodei to patch or de-deploy, and that Amodei "refused" — "the ball is in Anthropic's court." Semafor reports the real trigger was suspicion a China-linked group had accessed Mythos (flagged by Amazon's Jassy); Anthropic counters the White House "didn't raise Chinese access" in any of the jailbreak conversations and that it blocks access from inside China. Politico: the order followed "multiple tense calls" between Amodei and officials. The Information: the White House is unlikely to extend controls to other labs — undercutting the safety rationale.
    Why it's the story: The factual dispute is now the whole ballgame — either a frontier CEO knowingly kept a jailbroken cyberweapon live (Sacks), or Commerce nuked a deployed model over a minor, reproducible flaw to punish a disfavored company (Anthropic). That it won't be applied to OpenAI/GPT-5.5, which finds the same exploits, is the single most damning reportable detail. Press for: the actual statute cited, whether "export controls over US persons" survives legal scrutiny, and what telemetry backs the China-access claim.
  • A frontier model just triggered an international sovereignty panic — India and Europe call it a "wake-up call" TechCrunch AP The Fable suspension cut off Anthropic's own foreign-national employees and every overseas customer overnight. European political figures call it a "wake-up call" about depending on US-governed AI; in India it has reignited the sovereign-AI debate, with Sarvam reportedly raising fresh capital on the back of it.
    Why it's the story: This is the geopolitical second-order effect that mainstream coverage is still under-reporting — a single Commerce letter just became the strongest argument every non-US government has for building domestic frontier capacity. A clean explainer on how US export-control law can reach "US persons" and foreign customers of a cloud model, and what that means for sovereign-AI funding flows (Mistral's €3B raise, Sarvam), writes itself.
  • Data-center backlash is now a measurable drag: $130B in projects blocked or delayed in Q1 alone NBC News Ars Technica Data Center Watch: opponents blocked or delayed at least 75 US projects worth ~$130B in Jan–March — nearly matching all of 2025 in three months. Local opposition groups doubled to 833 across 49 states.
    Why it's the story: The buildout narrative (and the hyperscaler capex/equity-issuance story — Alphabet's ~$85B in planned offerings) rests on physical infrastructure that's increasingly getting stopped at the county level. Pair with last week's Crusoe Wyoming pause: the bottleneck on AI scaling may turn out to be zoning boards, not chips. Reportable: which projects, who's organizing, and whether opposition is bipartisan.
  • Employers blamed AI for 88K job cuts in 2026 so far — already 60% above all of 2025 Sherwood News Challenger, Gray & Christmas: of ~398K US cuts through May, employers cited AI for ~88K — up from ~54K in all of 2025, and nearly 40% of May's cuts.
    Why it's the story: A hard, climbing datapoint that cuts directly against Bezos's "AI brings golden ages, not job losses" framing (we covered June 11) and Amodei's UBI hedging. It's the empirical backbone for the labor-displacement beat — and the consent backdrop to OpenAI's retail-heavy mega-IPO. Worth interrogating how much "AI" is a euphemism cover for ordinary cost-cutting.
  • Russian families are using generative AI to "resurrect" relatives killed in Ukraine BBC A controversial trend sitting at the intersection of grief, the war, and consumer AI — animated likenesses and voices of dead soldiers.
    Why it's the story: A vivid, under-covered real-world harm/ethics case for the "AI and grief" thread (cf. the murder/suicide chatbot suits) — and one with a propaganda dimension, given the war context. A short explainer on griefbots, consent of the deceased, and where the model tooling comes from.
