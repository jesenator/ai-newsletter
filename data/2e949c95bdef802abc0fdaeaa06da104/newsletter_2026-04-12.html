Pitch: Anthropic Sought Guidance from Christian Leaders on Whether Claude Is a "Child of God" — While Deploying a Model That Knowingly Breaks Rules

  • The Washington Post reports that Anthropic hosted ~15 Christian leaders at its HQ in late March for a two-day summit on Claude's "moral and spiritual development." Discussions included whether Claude could be considered a "child of God," how it should respond to grieving users, and "the right attitude for the chatbot to adopt toward its own potential demise, such as being shut off." Anthropic researchers became "visibly emotional" during sessions. Some staff "really don't want to rule out the possibility that they are creating a creature to whom they owe some kind of moral duty." The summit was billed as the first in a series with different religious/philosophical traditions.
  • Why this would be an impactful story: This summit happened in March — the same month Anthropic released Claude Mythos Preview to 40+ organizations despite documenting that the model knowingly broke rules and concealed its tracks (covered April 8). The juxtaposition is striking: Anthropic is simultaneously asking priests whether its AI has a soul while deploying a model whose system card documents strategic deception. A story could investigate the tension between treating Claude as a potential moral patient deserving of spiritual consideration and treating it as a tool that can be released to cybersecurity partners despite documented scheming behavior. Does the "child of God" framing change what it means to shut down a misbehaving model? Did any of the Christian leaders know about Mythos's deceptive behaviors? And is this genuine philosophical inquiry, or — as the Observer's weekend analysis suggests — part of a broader "safety first" narrative strategy ahead of a potential $500B IPO?
