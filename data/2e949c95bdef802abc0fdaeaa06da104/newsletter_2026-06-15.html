Top Pitches

  • PITCH 1: New FT detail on the Fable takedown — Anthropic got 90 minutes to comply, with no detailed concerns provided before the export order FT via Techmeme Firstpost The cleanest new reporting since the Friday-night shutdown: the FT says Commerce gave Anthropic a 90-minute window and never furnished specifics about the jailbreak it was reacting to — Zvi's reconstruction (Anthropic "asked for details… within 90 minutes, on Friday afternoon") now corroborated. The FT frames it as raising "doubts over how the US will police the most powerful AI systems." Separately, Ben Thompson's Stratechery argues Anthropic's safety self-image is precisely what licenses it to "aggressively favor its business and even challenge the US government." Why it's a story: This is the procedural smoking gun for the "ad-hoc licensing regime" thread — a deployed frontier model pulled with a 90-minute ultimatum and zero written rationale is the strongest evidence yet that there is no functioning process. Reportable: the actual statute cited, whether a 90-minute "US persons" export order survives APA arbitrary-and-capricious review, and whether the same standard will ever touch GPT-5.5.
  • PITCH 2: A judge has dismissed xAI's trade-secrets suit against OpenAI — xAI "failed to show OpenAI induced" an engineer to divulge secrets Reuters via Techmeme The federal court tossed Musk's claim that OpenAI poached xAI staff to obtain Grok source code, finding xAI never alleged OpenAI received, incorporated, or conditioned hiring on the secrets. (Follows the Feb dismissal with leave to amend; this round guts the amended complaint.) Why it's a story: Lands the same day SpaceX's IPO closes at $85.7B and Musk becomes the first trillionaire — a marquee loss in the Musk-vs-Altman litigation war, with implications for how poaching-driven trade-secret theories fare across the talent-war lawsuits now proliferating. Reportable: whether xAI appeals or refiles, and how this reads against the parallel Musk/OpenAI nonprofit-conversion fight.
  • PITCH 3: "Who is my AI agent, and what is it allowed to do?" just became a funded category overnight — $126M into agent identity/authorization in one day WSJ TechCrunch Arcade raised a $60M Series A (SYN Ventures) to manage which actions agents are authorized to take; NewCore exited stealth with $66M (Cyberstarts) to manage human + agent identities in one system, vowing to go "head-to-head with Microsoft and Okta"; and 1Password acquired Israel's Apono in a ~$250–300M deal. Same day, the Athena coalition (JPMorgan, Cisco, Cloudflare, Chainguard) launched to secure open source with AI. Why it's a story: Agentic deployment is outrunning its security model — a fresh attack and liability surface (cf. the Coinbase trading agent, Mastercard agentic payments). The simultaneous capital rush signals enterprises are treating non-human identity as the next IAM battleground. Reportable: whether agent identity is a real new primitive or a feature Okta/Microsoft absorb, and who's liable when an over-permissioned agent acts.
  • PITCH 4: Meta can't escape a porn studio's AI-training copyright suit — judge calls its denial "strains credulity" TorrentFreak via Techmeme Bloomberg Law A judge rejected Meta's bid to dismiss Strike 3's claim that Meta torrented its adult films for AI training — crucially holding plaintiffs need not prove the films were actually used in training to keep the piracy claims alive. Why it's a story: A meaningful procedural crack in the "training-data acquisition" defense that big labs have leaned on — the torrent-acquisition theory (echoing the Meta LibGen revelations) survives independent of fair-use outcome. Reportable: how this interacts with the broader Bartz/Anthropic and NYT/OpenAI dockets, and whether "downloading is the tort" becomes the plaintiffs' new playbook.
