PITCH: Google's Pentagon Deal Forces DeepMind to Remove Its Own Safeguards — and Senior Leaders Are Publicly Calling It Indefensible

Google's Monday DoD contract doesn't just mirror OpenAI/xAI's "lawful use" framing — per Transformer's reporting, it explicitly "does not confer any right to control or veto lawful Government operational decision-making" and, per Institute for Law and AI's Charlie Bullock, appears to obligate Google to strip technical safeguards preventing the Pentagon from using Claude-equivalents for domestic mass surveillance or autonomous targeting — both of which can be "lawful under some circumstances." Over 600 employees signed a protest letter before the deal; 100+ lobbied Jeff Dean directly; leadership had told staff Google wouldn't cave. DeepMind research scientist Andreas Kirsch publicly: "I personally feel incredibly ashamed right now to be Senior Research Scientist at Google DeepMind." Alex Turner: "If OpenAI offered a fig leaf, Google said 'imagine we offered a fig leaf.'"

Why this is the story: This is the inverse of the Anthropic-Pentagon fight (which earned Anthropic a supply-chain-risk designation). Google had internal leadership opposition on record — Jeff Dean himself tweeted in February that "mass surveillance violates the Fourth Amendment." A reporter could (1) obtain the full internal letter and 100+ signatory list to Dean; (2) ask whether the "remove safeguards on lawful request" clause is unique to Google or now industry standard post-OpenAI/xAI; (3) probe Google's Project Maven history — workers killed it in 2018, then >24 Nimbus protesters were fired in 2024 — as a test of whether DeepMind's safety-hired talent still has leverage; (4) compare to today's parallel news that DoD just added Nvidia, Microsoft, Reflection AI, and AWS to the same "lawful operational use" framework. The 2025–26 fig-leaf regime for frontier models on classified networks is now locked in across every US lab except Anthropic.
