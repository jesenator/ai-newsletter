Pitch: Anduril's Manufacturing Operations Are Plagued by Safety Incidents and Dysfunction

  • Wired's 37-source investigation exposes safety incidents, management turnover, and deadline pressure at the $30.5B defense startup Wired A young engineer suffered burns assembling an electrical igniter — no job safety analysis had been conducted and his gloves weren't rated for fire protection. Local EMS was never called. His partner solicited donations on Facebook while the family lost its sole income. This is one of multiple safety incidents across Anduril's manufacturing lines. Sources describe "process dysfunction, management turnover, and deadline pressure beyond what they consider typical." Anduril declined to respond to specifics, calling the reporting "inaccurate or misleading" and threatening to "correct the record publicly."
  • Why this would be an impactful story Anduril is about to raise $4B more at a $60B valuation and is building Arsenal-1 in Ohio (4,000 jobs, $800M in state subsidies). It's pitching itself as the agile alternative to legacy defense. But if its "move fast" culture produces safety incidents at this stage — with only a few hundred Roadrunners and a handful of submarines produced — what happens when it's producing at the scale of a Lockheed Martin? The Wired piece has the granularity of a real investigative exposé: specific incidents, named factories, sourced from 37 people. The question a follow-up could ask: are Ohio regulators and the DOD tracking these incidents? And does Anduril's $800M in state grants require workplace safety compliance? This matters because Anduril is now one of the primary recipients of Anthropic's lost Pentagon business.
