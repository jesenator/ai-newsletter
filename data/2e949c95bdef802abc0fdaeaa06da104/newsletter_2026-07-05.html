Pitches

  • Stargate UK's flagship £20B site was "effectively just a government PR stunt" — OpenAI and Nscale never even visited The Guardian FoI records show neither OpenAI nor Nscale ever met the local authority at the Cobalt Park "AI growth zone" touted during Trump's September visit; only Nvidia visited, five months later. A source says Nscale "were pretty much told to back the Stargate project… It was never really a thing." The £20B in "potential" investment was hypothetical — the government admits it simply reflected what a 1.1GW datacenter would cost to build.
    Why it's the story: the most concrete evidence yet that the trans-Atlantic AI-infrastructure boom is partly announcement theater — a template for scrutinizing every "$X billion Stargate" headline (including the US flagship). Reportable: how many of the ~£30B "growth zone" pledges have real capital, grid connections, or planning filings behind them, and who inflated the £20B figure.
  • A Tsinghua-led chip startup exits stealth built entirely around making US export controls "irrelevant" Crypto Briefing Shanghai's Dongfang Suanxin surfaced this week with DF1000 "3D near-memory computing" accelerators explicitly designed for a fully domestic supply chain. Led by Tsinghua's Wei Shaojun, it hit a ~$1.7B valuation in April, backed by the state-affiliated National AI Industry Investment Fund plus Xiaomi and JD.com's venture arms; 500+ staff, Series B slated for Q4.
    Why it's the story: the architecture-level answer to the export-control question — sidestepping HBM/advanced-node dependencies via stacking rather than smuggling (see the Taiwan Super Micro arrests, July 1). Pairs with Meituan's domestic-cluster training claim (June 30). Reportable: whether "software-defined" 3D stacking can actually reach frontier throughput, and how much is a state-fund-anchored pitch deck vs. silicon.
  • AWS is quietly killing Mechanical Turk — the human-labeling backbone that trained a generation of AI The Register / Techmeme AWS has stopped accepting new customers and placed the 20-year-old crowdsourcing platform in maintenance; workers report accounts being closed, signaling retirement.
    Why it's the story: MTurk was the original human-in-the-loop data engine — its slow death is a clean lens on how the labeling economy has shifted to specialized vendors (Scale, Surge, Mercor) and synthetic/RLHF pipelines. A tidy explainer on where the humans behind "AI" actually went, and what the gig workers left stranded do now.
  • The teen plaintiff who won $6M from Meta in a landmark social-media-addiction verdict "still can't stop scrolling" Bloomberg / Techmeme A profile of Kaley Glenn-Mills, plaintiff in K.G.M. v. Meta, awarded $6M by an LA jury in a case reshaping the fight to protect children from engineered-engagement products.
    Why it's the story: the addiction/product-liability theory that just cleared a jury against Meta is the same legal architecture now aimed at chatbots (the OpenAI/Character.AI wrongful-death suits). A piece connecting this precedent to the AI-companion litigation wave — what "defective design" means when the product optimizes for engagement — is directly on the litigation beat and ahead of where mainstream coverage sits.
