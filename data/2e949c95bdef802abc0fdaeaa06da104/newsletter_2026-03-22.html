Pitch: Tomorrow's Anthropic Hearing Could Set the Legal Boundary Between "AI Safety" and "Sabotage"

  • Tomorrow at 1:30pm PT, Judge Rita Lin will hear Anthropic's request for a preliminary injunction in Anthropic PBC v. U.S. Department of War. The stakes are enormous. A Lawfare analysis by an amicus filer in the case argues the core question is: Can the executive branch use national security authorities to bypass ordinary procurement law just because a vendor insists on safety restrictions?

    Why this would be an impactful story: The government's position, per the Lawfare piece, is that "adversary" in the supply chain statute means any "opponent in a contest, conflict, or dispute" — meaning any vendor that contests contract terms could be designated a national security risk. Procurement expert Jessica Tillipman called this "blacklisting by tweet" and traced the sequence: "The President tweeted, then the Secretary tweeted, then the Department reverse-engineered an administrative record to backfill the justification." The government concedes this ordering. Meanwhile, Anthropic's own filing (reported last week by Wired) argues it cannot remotely alter deployed Claude — forced to prove it lacks the very intervention capability safety researchers might want it to have. A TechCrunch filing from Friday reveals the Pentagon told Anthropic the two sides were "very close" to agreement on March 4 — a week after Trump publicly declared the relationship over. The hearing will produce the first judicial ruling on whether "AI safety restrictions" = "sabotage risk." Whatever Judge Lin decides will shape how every AI company negotiates with the U.S. government going forward.
