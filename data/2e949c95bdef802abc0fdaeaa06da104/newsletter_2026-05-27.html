Top Pitches

PITCH 1: "BadHost" — A Single-Character Header Injection Breaks Auth on Millions of MCP Servers, vLLM, LiteLLM, and "Most OpenAI-Shim Proxies" Ars Technica MLQ CVE-2026-48710 in Starlette (325M downloads/week, base of FastAPI) lets unauthenticated attackers bypass path-based auth by injecting one character into the HTTP Host header. Secwest: the primitive "reaches a large segment of the Python AI tooling ecosystem: vLLM, LiteLLM, Text Generation Inference, most OpenAI-shim proxies, MCP servers, agent harnesses, eval dashboards, and model-management UIs." A public scanner at badhost.org went live, compressing the patch window. Severity rated 7/10 but X41 (discoverer) calls it "critical."
Why it's the story: This is the cleanest AI-agent-era supply-chain warning shot of 2026. MCP servers store credentials for every external system an agent touches — email, calendars, databases, payment rails. A reporter can (1) survey OpenAI/Anthropic/Google on whether their official MCP reference implementations were vulnerable and when they patched; (2) check if any of Anthropic's Project Glasswing "10,000 critical vulns" findings were Starlette-adjacent; (3) press Robinhood (which today let users connect Claude/Cursor to live trading accounts — see below) on whether its agent-broker bridge runs Starlette; (4) tie to the killed Trump EO, which would have established a CISA clearinghouse for exactly this class of bug. Pairs with the Megalodon GitHub Actions worm and Nx Console attacks — the AI-agent attack surface is hardening faster than defenders.
PITCH 2: Robinhood Now Lets Claude and Cursor Trade Stocks Autonomously — On the Same Day a Critical MCP Auth-Bypass Drops WSJ Robinhood launched a feature letting customers link AI agents (Claude, Cursor named explicitly) to dedicated, segregated investment accounts and credit-card accounts for fully autonomous trading and purchases. WSJ frames this as a routine product launch.
Why it's the story: This is the first US retail brokerage to give consumer-grade LLM agents live market access at scale, and it lands the same morning as the BadHost MCP disclosure. Worth pressing: (1) what circuit-breakers exist if Claude hallucinates a ticker or Cursor's agent loop runs a flash-crash sequence; (2) FINRA/SEC posture — agentic trading by a non-registered "advisor" is a regulatory novelty; (3) compare against Anthropic's own usage policy (which historically barred autonomous financial decisions); (4) does Robinhood's bridge use Starlette/FastAPI? The bigger frame: agents are crossing into regulated-money rails with no statutory clarity on liability when they err. The Spain-blocked Polymarket story and Wired's piece on YC's six AI-debt-collection startups suggest a pattern: consumer-financial-regulated verticals are where agentic AI is racing furthest ahead of the law.
PITCH 3: Transformer's Verdict on Pope Leo's Encyclical — "Reads Like an AI Ethics Paper From 2022" Transformer Shakeel Hashim's analysis: Magnifica Humanitas "does not even discuss the concept of 'artificial general intelligence,' nor does he acknowledge the many catastrophic risks that leading scientists believe such technology poses." Damningly, Anthropic's Chris Olah at the launch event "spoke of the need for a global redistributive mechanism more clearly than Leo did." The Vatican's January 2025 Antiqua et Nova was tougher; the binding doctrine is softer.
Why it's the story: Companion to Zvi's Tuesday read (Olah dissented on paragraph 99). The takeaway hardens: Anthropic got the lobbying win in optics (Olah on stage, Meta/Google/Amazon shut out) but the encyclical they helped shape is materially weaker on existential risk than the Vatican's own prior document. A reporter could ask Olah on the record whether Anthropic is satisfied with how AGI/loss-of-control got cut, and whether Paolo Benanti (Vatican AI advisor who in 2025 said superintelligence "cannot be permitted to proceed without sufficient oversight") was sidelined in the drafting process.
