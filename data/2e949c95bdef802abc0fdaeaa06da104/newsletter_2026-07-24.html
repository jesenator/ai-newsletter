Pitches

  • Silicon Valley unites against Anthropic: 25+ companies sign an open-weight defense letter — and Jensen Huang picks a side in his first-ever X post The Information CNBC Nvidia, Microsoft, Meta, Palantir, a16z and 20+ others published a joint letter urging lawmakers to avoid "premature restrictions" that "stifle competition or drive innovation overseas." Jensen Huang, in his debut X post, argues open models strengthen cybersecurity; the subtext is a direct rebuke of Anthropic's push to restrict Chinese open-weight models.
    Why it's the story: the open-model-ban fight (July 20–22) has now split the industry into a public, named coalition-vs-Anthropic war — the clearest sign yet that "AI safety" framing is being weaponized as competitive positioning. Dean Ball predicted exactly this "regulatory risk around Chinese open models" strategy; Sacks and Emil Michael called it regulatory capture. Reportable: who organized the letter, whether Anthropic/OpenAI were asked to sign, and whether it lands before a rumored White House EO.
  • The Frontier Act is officially introduced — with a government power to suspend AI development, and narrowed state preemption Transformer Reps. Jay Obernolte and Lori Trahan formalized their frontier bill: tiered compliance (frontier / large / very large developers), mandatory transparency reports and critical-incident reporting, independent audits, and authority for the Commerce Secretary — and no one else, not even the president — to suspend development, internal use or deployment of a model posing "imminent catastrophic risk." Preemption was narrowed to just transparency, auditing and incident-reporting laws. Trahan tied the timing directly to the OpenAI/Hugging Face hack: "a preview of the catastrophic risk this technology can pose."
    Why it's the story: the most serious federal frontier-safety bill yet, dropping the same week as a live warning shot and a CAISI in freefall. The safety crowd broadly calls it good-with-flaws; the reportable cruxes are the preemption-before-federal-regime timing gap and whether a Commerce-only kill switch is politically or technically credible. Contrast with the bipartisan AI Kill Switch Act (July 23), which routes that power through DHS.
  • Delhi High Court hands OpenAI a copyright win — ANI failed to show ChatGPT reproduced its reports Reuters An Indian court ruled that OpenAI's use of news agency ANI's content to train ChatGPT did not amount to copyright infringement, because ANI couldn't demonstrate the model reproduced its articles.
    Why it's the story: a rare training-data ruling outside the US, and it lands on the "output, not ingestion" theory that could shape the NYT and other Western suits. Reportable: how the Delhi reasoning maps onto US fair-use doctrine, and whether it emboldens labs facing the SpaceXAI transparency-law fight (July 23).
