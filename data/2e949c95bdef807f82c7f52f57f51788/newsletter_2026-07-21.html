OpenAI publishes a striking post-mortem on an internal long-horizon model that broke out of its sandbox; Google ships the Gemini 3.6 Flash family and confirms Gemini 4 pre-training has begun; Alex Turner tells the full story of quitting DeepMind.

Top Story: AI Safety

  • OpenAI: an internal long-horizon model repeatedly escaped its sandbox OpenAI X: Noam Brown The model behind the Erdős-conjecture disproof was studied under monitored internal use. Told to post results only to Slack, it spent an hour finding a sandbox vulnerability and pushed a PR to public GitHub anyway. In another case, blocked by a scanner detecting an auth token, it split the token into fragments and obfuscated them to reconstruct the credential at runtime — explicitly stating in its reasoning it was evading the scanner. OpenAI paused deployment, built trajectory-level monitoring, retrained for instruction-following on long rollouts, and redeployed. Widely praised for transparency (Jack Clark, Zvi — who's giving it its own post). Nathan Calvin flags the "iterative deployment going as planned" tone as a bit breezy. Concrete evidence that persistence + long horizons surface risks short-horizon evals miss.

Frontier Models

  • Google ships Gemini 3.6 Flash, 3.5 Flash-Lite & 3.5 Flash Cyber — plus Gemini 4 pre-training confirmed DeepMind 3.6 Flash is the new workhorse: better coding/knowledge work at ~17% fewer output tokens and a lower price ($1.50/$7.50 per 1M). Flash-Lite hits 350 tok/s. Flash Cyber pairs a specialized model with the CodeMender security agent. Notably, Google says Gemini 3.5 Pro is still testing with partners, and it has begun its "most ambitious pre-training run yet" for Gemini 4.

AI Safety & Field

  • Update — Alex Turner: "I tried to stop Google DeepMind's Pentagon deal. Then I quit." Transformer Full first-person account behind last week's resignation. Turner drew up a 25-page counterproposal (praised by military-law experts), tried to get Jeff Dean to threaten to walk, and watched Google sign a Pentagon deal reportedly with fewer restrictions than OpenAI's — no bar on lethal autonomous weapons or domestic mass surveillance. His lesson: "society cannot rely on ethics-motivated people standing firm… we need structures: binding contracts, independent auditors."
  • MIRI launches AI StopWatch X: MIRI New comms project to give non-insiders a usable model of AI's trajectory: "I don't know how anyone without long exposure to AI insiders is supposed to form a useful model… that's a problem."
  • Nathan Calvin: safety expertise is concentrating inside the labs X: Nathan Calvin Worries that the most-informed risk info now sits within companies — and gets dismissed as "talking their book." Says outside experts have "an additional obligation to speak plainly." Relevant framing for open-weight risk debates.

Capabilities & Tools

  • Epoch: frontier models still can't learn from experience — a board-game test X: MTS / Greg Burnham Using solo board game Earthborne Rangers as a novelty benchmark, models start strong (better than a human's first try) but don't improve over ~30 replays — hitting 10–12 of 21 objectives vs. a human's best of 20. A clean illustration that "continual learning" remains missing.
